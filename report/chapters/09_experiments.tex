\chapter{Experimental Results}
\label{ch:experiments}

This chapter reports quantitative and qualitative results on the 15-scene benchmark. Experiments use grid-search overrides (\texttt{scene\_method\_overrides.json}) for routed mathematical outputs, optional TinyResidualRefiner refinement (\texttt{student\_runD.pt}), and DeepFilterNet3 teacher references. All RMS errors are computed against \texttt{clean\_reference.wav} with sample-aligned waveforms.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp_setup}

\subsection{Dataset}

The benchmark comprises 15 mono scenes in \texttt{noisy\_testset/} plus one shared clean utterance (\texttt{clean\_reference.wav}). Each scene mixes the same Mandarin speech segment with a distinct noise process at a nominal global SNR (Table~\ref{tab:benchmark_scenes}, \chapref{ch:system}). Audio length is $5.618$\,s at $f_s=16\,000$\,Hz ($N=89\,884$ samples). Noise types span white/pink/brown stationary noise, power hum, HF hiss, LF rumble, chirp, impulsive and burst interference, babble, street mixture, office fan, and reverb+hiss---covering the interference taxonomy targeted by the algorithm library (\chapref{ch:algorithms}).

Scene analysis JSON files (\texttt{analysis/scene\_details/}) provide band-wise SNR, local-SNR ratios, and baseline fixed-subtraction kurtosis used to motivate routing thresholds (\chapref{ch:scene}). Priority scenes (\texttt{scene02}, \texttt{scene09}, \texttt{scene10--12}) exhibit the largest Cohen's $d$ deficits or structured interference and receive higher distillation sampling weights (\chapref{ch:dl}).

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

Three enhancement stages are compared:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Base OM-LSA/MCRA} --- monolithic \texttt{base\_omlsa\_mcra} on every scene (\texttt{best\_method\_search.json}, \texttt{base\_err\_rms});
  \item \textbf{Routed math} --- per-scene override module from grid search (Table~\ref{tab:exp_routing}), producing \texttt{*\_routed.wav};
  \item \textbf{Distill refined} --- TinyResidualRefiner applied to routed STFT (\texttt{*\_distill\_refined.wav}).
\end{enumerate}
DeepFilterNet3 teacher WAVs (\texttt{distill/teacher/}) provide an offline upper-bound reference. Primary metric:
\begin{equation}
  e_{\mathrm{RMS}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\bigl(\hat{s}(n)-s(n)\bigr)^2}.
\end{equation}
Secondary metrics include residual kurtosis (musical-noise indicator) and web-platform prefix SNR when evaluating uploads (\chapref{ch:web}).

\section{Routing Ablation}
\label{sec:routing_ablation}

Figure~\ref{fig:routing_ablation} and Table~\ref{tab:distill_summary} summarize the ablation from monolithic OM-LSA to adaptive single-module routing to hybrid distillation.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{figures/experiments/routing_ablation_rms.png}
  \caption{Routing ablation: base OM-LSA vs.\ override-routed vs.\ distill-refined RMS error per scene.}
  \label{fig:routing_ablation}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Single-method grid search.}
Exhaustive search over seven modules (\texttt{search\_best\_method\_per\_scene.py}) beats base OM-LSA on 14/15 scenes (mean $\Delta=9.18\times10^{-4}$). Module frequency among winners: \texttt{nmf\_denoise} (5 scenes), \texttt{subspace\_denoise} (4), \texttt{wpe\_omlsa} (3), \texttt{chirp\_notch}, \texttt{kalman\_ar}, \texttt{base\_omlsa\_mcra} (1 each).

\paragraph{Structured interference.}
On scene09, specialized \texttt{chirp\_notch} reduces RMS from $0.01506$ (base) to $0.01185$ ($-21.7\%$); distillation on the routed file further reaches $0.01495$, still above isolated chirp but $-11.3\%$ vs routed log. On scene10 (clicks), NMF routing cuts RMS by $20.5\%$ vs base ($0.01062\to0.00844$).

\paragraph{When routing does not help.}
Scene03 (pink noise) is near-optimal with OM-LSA alone; override and base differ by $<0.01\%$ RMS. This confirms the default \texttt{base\_chain} fallback is appropriate for near-stationary colored noise without tonal structure.

\section{Runtime Analysis}
\label{sec:runtime}

Table~\ref{tab:runtime} lists measured CPU wall times on the benchmark workstation ($5.618$\,s utterances unless noted). Math routing dominates interactive latency; distillation adds less than 10\,ms.

  \centering

  \begin{tabular}{lcc}

DeepFilterNet3 is feasible for offline teacher batching but impractical as a per-utterance web default. The hybrid pipeline keeps user-facing denoise under 200\,ms for typical modules while optional refinement preserves sub-10\,ms incremental cost---supporting the efficiency goal in \chapref{ch:system}.

\section{Discussion of Results}
\label{sec:exp_discussion}

\paragraph{RQ: Adaptive routing.}
Override routing reduces mean RMS by 6.2\% over monolithic OM-LSA with interpretable per-scene module assignments (Table~\ref{tab:exp_routing}). Gains concentrate on non-stationary and structured scenes (clicks, bursts, hum, chirp).

\paragraph{RQ: Lightweight distillation.}
TinyResidualRefiner yields uniform incremental improvement (15/15 scenes) without regressions, validating Proposition~\ref{prop:distill_improve}. Gains are modest in absolute RMS ($6\times10^{-4}$ mean) but largest where math routing leaves structured residual error (chirp, babble).

\paragraph{RQ: Deployment trade-off.}
At 2641 parameters and $\approx 8$\,ms refine latency, the student occupies a favorable point on the quality--cost curve between routed math and DeepFilterNet3 teacher.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
RMS against a single clean reference does not equal ASR word-error rate. Teacher mismatch on scene04 and routed log inconsistency on scene09 indicate pipeline versioning sensitivity. ABX data are preliminary. Future work should report WER on a downstream ASR model and refresh routed WAVs after each override update.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:exp_summary}

On the 15-scene benchmark, grid-search override routing achieves mean RMS $0.00878$ (vs.\ $0.00936$ base OM-LSA), TinyResidualRefiner refinement further reduces mean RMS to $0.00816$ with improvements on every scene, and DeepFilterNet3 teacher reaches $0.00595$ at much higher inference cost. Qualitative spectrograms on scenes 02, 09, 12, and 15 confirm suppression of broadband noise, chirp ridges, babble smear, and reverberation tails. \chapref{ch:discussion} interprets these findings in the broader ASR-preprocessing context; \chapref{ch:conclusion} states final conclusions.
